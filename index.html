In U.S. News scandal, a confession, an apology, and previously unreleased data emerge

By Irie Sentner and Zachary Schermele

September 10, 2022

Photo by Princeton Huang

After months of criticism and confusion, University Provost Mary Boyce admitted Friday evening that the University misreported data to U.S. News & World Report in its submission to the publication’s 2021 Best Colleges ranking, confirming allegations first leveled by mathematics professor Michael Thaddeus in February.

Boyce also publicly released, for the first time, the University’s Common Data Set, a trove of statistical information that is used to inform school rankings. Until now, Columbia was the only Ivy League university to not publicly release such data and also appears to be the only Ivy to become embroiled in a misreporting scandal, although many other colleges and universities have misreported to U.S. News in the past.

Columbia’s ranking in the 2021 list boosted the University to an unprecedented level of prestige—a three-way tie as the second-best school in the country alongside Harvard University and the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and behind only Princeton University. The No. 2 ranking was frequently touted by the University.

It is clear now, officially, that Columbia’s No. 2 ranking was based in part on information that was factually incorrect.

In an announcement to the University community, Boyce wrote that her office “had previously relied on outdated and/or incorrect methodologies” when collecting and submitting data about average class sizes and the amount of faculty with terminal degrees, which are often, but not always, doctorates. It’s still unclear what those methodologies were and who exactly is directly responsible for the mistakes.

The validity of the data was first questioned in a high-profile way last spring, when Thaddeus published an analysis on a Columbia-hosted site that alleged inconsistencies in the University’s representation of information about its undergraduate course sizes, student-to-faculty-ratio, and graduation outcomes, among other data.

Boyce acknowledged that class size data was previously reported incorrectly and “not in compliance with the U.S. News instructions.” The “prior methodologies,” she said, “caused the University to overreport the number of classes with under 20 students and underreport classes with between 20 and 29 students.”

The Common Data Set now shows that in fall 2021, 57 percent of undergraduate classes had enrollments of under 20 students, and about three quarters of classes had enrollments of under 25. In his analysis, Thaddeus said that the University had previously reported that 82.5 percent of its undergraduate classes had under 20 students.

“Provost Boyce, by attributing the discrepancy to ‘outdated and/or incorrect methodologies,’ does serious damage to her own credibility,” Thaddeus said in a statement to Spectator on Friday.

The Common Data Set also now puts the number of full-time faculty with “terminal degrees,” which are usually doctorates, at 95 percent. Thaddeus said the University previously reported 100 percent.

“We deeply regret the deficiencies in our prior reporting and are committed to doing better,” Boyce said in the Friday announcement. She added that the University will “continue to refine and review” its methodologies and that a third-party advisory firm, the Ankura Consulting Group, reviewed the new Common Data Set

Class sizes and faculty education levels are two factors of campus life that have long been at the center of conversations about the quality of education that Columbia offers its undergraduates.

The University promises students close access to “highly accomplished faculty” through “small discussion-based classes,” particularly through the Core Curriculum. It’s a pledge that has come under scrutiny in recent years as the Faculty of Arts and Sciences, which is responsible for fulfilling the promise of the Core Curriculum, has come up against budgetary challenges and space constraints.

These criticisms came to a head last year amid classroom shortages and overenrolled courses, when about 9 percent more undergraduate students were enrolled in 2021 than the year prior due to administrators’ uncertainty about how the pandemic would affect admitted student yield, and because of deferrals and leaves of absence. At the same time, administrators were considering enrolling even more students, an effort that was eventually postponed, for the most part, after faculty raised concerns.

In the months following Thaddeus’ allegations, Boyce said her office conducted an “extensive and time consuming” assessment of its collection and submission practices. That review was conducted largely behind closed doors, and some faculty believe that University administrators have not been transparent enough about what had gone wrong, eight current faculty members confirmed to Spectator.

Those faculty members called the University’s misreporting on the whole concerning. Over half of them also said they felt the scandal, which has now dragged on for months amid intense administrative turnover and a search for the next University president, has damaged the University’s reputation.

On Friday, the University for the first time published two Common Data Sets. The data, compiled in separate documents for Columbia College and the School of Engineering and Applied Science, and for the School of General Studies, which has a separate admissions office, offers a comprehensive overview of key characteristics of the schools, including new information about the University’s highly secretive undergraduate admissions processes.

The data provides an overview of the University’s official admissions criteria. For Columbia College and SEAS, rigor of secondary school record, class rank, academic GPA, application essay, and recommendations are all considered “very important,” while standardized test scores—which were made optional during the pandemic—are “considered.” For General Studies—which is also test-optional—standardized test scores, rigor of secondary school record, academic GPA, and the application essay are all “very important,” while class rank and recommendations are “important.”

Although Columbia College, SEAS, and General Studies all had test-optional admissions policies during the cycle, among Columbia College and SEAS applicants, 44 percent chose to submit SAT scores and 29 percent chose to submit ACT scores. Among General Studies applicants, those numbers dropped to 26 percent and 17 percent, respectively.

For Columbia College and SEAS, extracurricular activities and “[c]haracter/personal qualities” are “very important” and “[t]alent/ability” is “important.” The interview, volunteer work, and work experience are “considered,” as are an applicant’s status as a first-generation college student, relation to alumni, geographical residence, and race and ethnicity.

For General Studies, the interview, work experience, first-generation status, and “[c]haracter/personal qualities” are “very important,” as is the level of applicant interest, which Columbia College and SEAS do not consider. Extracurricular activities, “[t]alent/ability,” and race and ethnicity are “important,” and alumni relation, geographical residence, state residence, and volunteer work are “considered.”

The higher education community more broadly has long upheld yield, or the percentage of admitted students who ultimately enroll, as a signal of institutional prestige. Of the 2,355 students accepted to Columbia College and SEAS, 1,560–or 66 percent–enrolled. Of the 150 students admitted to General Studies, 81–or 54 percent–enrolled.

The University did not disclose the number of students placed on and accepted from the waitlist during the 2020-21 admissions cycle.

During the 2020-21 admissions cycle, 35,406 women and 25,145 men applied to Columbia College and SEAS, but 1,220 women and 1,135 men were admitted, making the total acceptance rate 4.5 percent for men and 3.4 percent for women.

Women applying during that cycle were admitted at a rate of nearly 24 percent less than men.

For General Studies, 232 women and 308 men applied during fall 2021, with 28 percent of men and 27 percent of women gaining admission. The bulk of applicants were transfers, which in the fall included 724 men transfer applicants, 422 of whom were accepted, and 696 women transfer applicants for women, 400 of whom were accepted.

Last year, Columbia College and SEAS awarded nearly $190 million in institutional need-based financial aid, while General Studies awarded about $31 million, the data sets show. Nearly 100 percent of need was met for full-time students in Columbia College and SEAS who received need-based aid. Only about half was met for students in General Studies.

Despite potentially higher overall attendance costs, School of General Studies students receive significantly less financial aid than other Columbia undergraduates

For the 2021-22 school year, the cost of attendance for General Studies was over $8,000 more expensive than Columbia College and the School of Engineering and Applied Science. However, financial aid packages for its students still lag behind the college and SEAS, with General Studies students having received $20,000 less in aid on average than Columbia College and SEAS students. This gap increases to almost $40,000 when only looking at first-years.

Among students at Columbia College and SEAS, 87 percent live in University housing and 13 percent commute. Just 31 percent of General Studies students live in University housing and 69 percent commute. The Common Data Sets diverge in their estimated costs for students living off campus and not at home, with Columbia College and SEAS commuter students estimated to spend $16,212 on room and board compared to $24,030 for those attending General Studies.

Among a cohort of students who first enrolled in 2015, Columbia College and SEAS had a 95 percent rate of students who graduated within six years. That number drops to 93 percent for students who received a Pell grant. The General Studies rate is 97 percent, and 100 percent of students receiving Pell grants graduated during that time. Nearly 10 percent of General Studies students in that cohort received Pell grants, compared to 17 percent of Columbia College and SEAS students.
